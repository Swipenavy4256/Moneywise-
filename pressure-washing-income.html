How Much Do Pressure Washers Actually Make?

By MoneyWise · Last updated July 22, 2026 · 7 min read

Pressure washing gets hyped constantly as an easy six-figure business. The truth is more interesting: it really is one of the highest-paying side hustles per hour you can start — but the income claims floating around social media skip all the real costs and realities. Here are the actual numbers.

What Pressure Washers Charge Per Job

Typical residential pricing (varies by region)

Driveway (2-car)$150–$350
House wash (single story)$250–$400
House wash (two story)$350–$600

Roof cleaning (soft wash)$400–$800

A driveway takes a skilled operator 1–2 hours. That's why the per-hour math looks so good: even at the low end, you're earning $75–150/hour of actual work. The catch — and it's a big one — is that you're not working every hour. Quoting, driving, marketing, and waiting for callbacks fill the gaps.

Achievable after 6–12 months of building reviews and repeat customers. The operators actually hitting these numbers spend as much time on marketing and quotes as they do washing. Also note "in season" — in most of the country, work drops hard from November to March.

Reality check: The "$1,000/day pressure washing" videos you see usually feature commercial contracts (shopping centers, HOAs, fleets) that took years of relationship-building to land — or they're selling a course. Solo residential operators starting out should expect $200–400 per full day of booked work.

What It Costs to Start

  • Pressure washer (the actual machine): $300–700 for a gas unit with at least 3,000 PSI. Skip electric units for paid work — they're too slow.
  • Surface cleaner attachment: $100–200. Cuts driveway time in half and leaves no streaks. Non-negotiable for pro results.
  • Hoses, nozzles, downstream injector: $100–150
  • General liability insurance: $40–80/month. You're spraying water at 3,000 PSI near windows, cars, and siding — one mistake without insurance can erase a year of profit.

All in, a serious starter kit runs $600–$1,500. You can test the business for even less by renting a machine ($40–100/day) for your first few paid jobs before buying anything.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do you need a license to pressure wash?

Most areas only require a standard business license, but some cities and states have specific rules about wastewater runoff. Check your city's requirements — it usually takes one phone call. Insurance matters more than licensing for protecting yourself.

Is pressure washing hard on your body?

It's real physical work — you're on your feet in the heat handling equipment that kicks back. It's easier than roofing or moving furniture, harder than desk work. Most people can handle 2–3 jobs a day.

Is the market oversaturated?

In most suburbs, no — demand for exterior cleaning consistently outpaces reliable operators. The businesses that fail usually fail at showing up and quoting promptly, not at finding customers.
