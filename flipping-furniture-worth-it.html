Is Flipping Furniture Worth It in 2026?

By MoneyWise · Last updated July 22, 2026 · 7 min read

Furniture flipping — buying used pieces cheap, fixing them up, and reselling — exploded during the pandemic and never fully cooled down. But with more flippers in the market and social media making it look effortless, the real question is whether it still pays in 2026. Short answer: yes, for a specific type of person. Here's the honest math.

The Real Numbers Per Flip

A typical solid-wood dresser flip

Buy price (Marketplace, estate sale)$0–$60
Supplies (paint, hardware, sandpaper)$25–$50
Hours of work4–8 hrs
Sale price$200–$450
Typical profit$125–$350

Spread over the hours involved (including pickup, photography, and messaging buyers), most flips work out to $20–45/hour. Good pieces in wealthy suburbs skew higher; painted particle board skews toward zero.

What Actually Sells (And What Doesn't)

✅ Buy These Every Time

Solid wood dressers are the kings of flipping — always in demand, easy to refinish, and they hold value. Also reliable: nightstand pairs, mid-century anything, small desks, cedar chests, and real-wood bookshelves. The test: if it's heavy and the drawers are dovetailed, it's probably worth money.

❌ Walk Away From These

Particle board and IKEA-style furniture (worth almost nothing refinished), entertainment centers (nobody wants them anymore), sleeper sofas (heavy, low demand), glass-top dining sets, and anything with water damage that's swollen the wood. Also skip upholstered pieces unless you know how to clean them properly — buyers are wary of bugs and odors.

The #1 beginner mistake: Buying anything cheap just because it's cheap. A free entertainment center that takes 10 hours to refinish and sells for $50 is a worse deal than a $60 dresser that takes 5 hours and sells for $400. Buy for the resale value, not the purchase price.

Monthly Income Scenarios

  • Casual (1–2 flips/month): $150–$500/month. Perfect if you enjoy the work — it's a paid hobby.
  • Consistent (4–6 flips/month): $600–$1,500/month. Requires garage space and weekend commitment.
  • Serious (10+ flips/month): $2,000–$3,500/month. This is a part-time job with batching, a paint sprayer, and a trailer or truck.

What You Need to Start

  • Orbital sander: $30–60 — the single most important tool
  • Paint and topcoat: $40–70 per few pieces (cabinet-grade paint is worth it)
  • Basics: screwdrivers, wood filler, cleaning supplies — $30
  • A vehicle that fits a dresser — or a friend with one (this is genuinely the biggest constraint)

Total: about $100–200 to be fully equipped. Your first piece can often be found free on curbs or the "free" section of Facebook Marketplace.

Where the money really is: photography and listings. A $300 dresser and a $150 dresser are often the same dresser — one was photographed in good light against a clean wall with styled decor, the other in a dark garage. So — Worth It or Not?

Worth it if: you have garage or patio space, a way to move furniture, and you actually enjoy hands-on work. The margins are real, the startup cost is tiny compared to equipment-heavy hustles like pressure washing, and demand for quality used furniture keeps growing as new furniture prices climb.

Not worth it if: you need money this week (flips take days to sell — see faster options here), you live in a small apartment, or you'd resent spending Saturdays sanding. The people who quit usually quit because of logistics, not profit.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a piece take to sell?

Well-photographed, fairly priced pieces in metro areas typically sell in 3–10 days on Facebook Marketplace. If nothing's moving in two weeks, the price or photos are the problem.

Do I need to know woodworking?

No. The majority of profitable flips are clean → sand → paint → new hardware. YouTube can teach the entire skill set in a weekend. Repairs beyond wobbly legs and sticky drawers usually aren't worth taking on.

Is the market oversaturated with flippers?

More people flip now than five years ago, but demand grew too. Flippers with good photos and fair prices still sell consistently — the low-effort spray-paint flippers are the ones struggling.
