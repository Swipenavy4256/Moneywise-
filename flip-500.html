I Had $500 and Needed to Make More. Here's What I Did.

June 29, 2026 · 8 min read · by MoneyWise
Most money articles talk about long-term investing and waiting 30 years. Great. But what if you need money, like, soon? Here's what actually works when you've got $500 and need to turn it into more — fast.

First, Let's Be Honest About What $500 Can and Can't Do

Five hundred dollars is not going to make you rich overnight. Anyone who tells you that is trying to sell you something. But $500 is actually a pretty solid amount to start with — way more than most people think.

The key is not treating it like pocket money. You put it to work. Here are the best options, ranked by how fast you can see returns.

Quick Note

These aren't get-rich-quick schemes. They're real things real people do. Some take more work than others, but none of them require any special skills or connections to get started.

Option 1: Flip Items for Profit

📦 Buy Low, Sell Higher

$0–$500
1–3 days
Low

This is probably the most straightforward way to start. You find stuff that's being sold for cheap — like on Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, or thrift stores — and you sell it for more on eBay or back on Marketplace.

The trick is knowing what to look for. Electronics, tools, gaming stuff, name-brand furniture, and sneakers are the big ones. People sell them cheap all the time because they just want it gone. You come in, clean it up, take better photos, write a better description, and sell it for 2–3x what you paid.

I've seen people flip a $40 lawn mower for $150. A $20 Xbox controller for $60. A $50 couch for $200. It adds up fast, and once you do it a few times you get a feel for what's worth picking up.

With $500, you could buy and flip 5–10 items at a time. That's a real mini-business right there. Check out our guide to flipping on Facebook Marketplace for the specific items to target.

Option 2: Start a Pressure Washing Business

🧹 Spray Things, Get Paid

$150–$400
3–7 days
Very Low

You can get a decent starter pressure washer for around $150. Then you go around your neighborhood and offer to clean driveways, patios, and house siding. People pay $80–$200 per job. That's your $150 back after 1–2 jobs, pure profit after that.

This one is sneaky good because the competition is usually low (most people don't want to do physical work), the demand is constant (driveways get dirty every year), and customers who like you will call you back every spring and summer.

Option 3: Retail Arbitrage

🛒 Buy From Stores, Sell Online

$200–$500
1–2 weeks
Low–Medium

Retail arbitrage is when you buy clearance or discounted items from regular stores — Walmart, Target, HomeGoods, TJ Maxx — and then list them on Amazon for more.

There's an app called the Amazon Seller app that lets you scan barcodes in the store and instantly see what the item sells for on Amazon. If the difference is big enough to make a profit after Amazon's fees, you buy it. Simple as that.

The learning curve is a little higher than pure flipping, but $500 can get you started with a solid inventory batch. Plenty of sellers make $1,000–3,000 a month once they figure out what sells.

Option 4: Offer a Local Service

Here's the thing about services — you don't necessarily need to spend all $500 upfront. Most service businesses need only $50–200 in basic tools. Think about what's in demand around you:

  • Lawn mowing — $25–50 per lawn, basic mower can be rented or bought used
  • Car detailing — $80–200 per car, supplies are cheap
  • Junk removal — charge by truckload, people pay a lot to get rid of stuff
  • Window cleaning — simple, cheap to start, good repeat business
  • Power washing — covered above, great margins

The beauty here is you use the $500 to buy supplies and pocket the rest. As you grow, you reinvest. Most of these can turn into real businesses if you want them to.

Option 5: Learn a Skill and Freelance

This one takes a little longer but has the highest ceiling. If you don't already have a marketable skill, $500 can buy you one. Video editing, graphic design, copywriting, bookkeeping, coding basics — there are online courses for all of these, many under $50.

Once you have even a basic skill, you can find clients on Upwork, Fiverr, or just by reaching out directly to small businesses. You don't need to be an expert. You just need to be good enough to do the job.

The first client is the hardest. After that, things tend to snowball.

Bottom Line

If you want money fast: flip items or start a pressure washing business. Both can have money in your pocket within a week. If you want something with more upside long-term: retail arbitrage or freelancing. And if you're patient: invest and let it grow.

What About Just Investing the $500?

Totally fine to do, but be realistic. $500 in an index fund might turn into $600 or $700 over a couple of years if things go well. That's fine, but it's not going to change your day-to-day financial situation.

If you need money now, investing is not the answer. If you already have money coming in and want to build long-term wealth, then yes, throw that $500 into a Roth IRA or an S&P 500 index fund and forget about it.

The real answer is usually: make money first, then invest the profits.
